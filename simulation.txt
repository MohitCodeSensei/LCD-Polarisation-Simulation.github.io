Description of Activity:For this activity, I developed a computer-based interactive HTML/JS simulation demonstrating the real-world application of Polarization and Malus's Law in Liquid Crystal Displays (LCDs).Beyond the Course Content Justification:While the course syllabus covers the fundamental theory of polarization, this simulation moves beyond basic principles to model how crossed polarizers and twisted nematic liquid crystals are engineered together to actively control light transmission in modern digital displays.Explanation of Theory (Physics behind the activity):An LCD pixel operates as a light valve using polarized light. The simulation models the following continuous process:Initial Polarization: Unpolarized light from a backlight passes through a rear polarizing filter, becoming linearly polarized (e.g., vertically).The "Twisted Nematic" Effect: Between the rear and front polarizers sits a layer of liquid crystals. When no voltage is applied (Pixel ON state), the molecules naturally form a helical structure that mechanically twists the polarization angle of the passing light by exactly $90^\circ$.Transmission: The light, now horizontally polarized, successfully passes through the front horizontal polarizing filter, illuminating the pixel on the screen.Malus's Law and Voltage Application: When an electric voltage is applied (Pixel OFF state), the liquid crystals untwist and align with the electric field. They no longer rotate the light's polarization angle. According to Malus's Law ($I = I_0 \cos^2 \theta$), because the angle between the vertically polarized light and the horizontal transmission axis of the front filter is $90^\circ$, the transmitted intensity becomes zero ($\cos 90^\circ = 0$). The light is entirely blocked, and the pixel appears dark.
